Nuclear fusion is increasingly seen as the potential savior for nuclear energy, offering a clean, virtually limitless source of power by replicating the processes that fuel the sun. Unlike nuclear fission, which splits atoms and produces long-lived radioactive waste, fusion combines hydrogen isotopes to form helium, generating immense energy with minimal waste and no risk of a catastrophic meltdown. Collaborative efforts such as the International Thermonuclear Experimental Reactor (ITER), involving 35 countries including the United States, China, Russia, India, and the European Union, highlight the global commitment to harnessing fusion\u2019s potential. Fusion's advantages include abundant fuel sources like deuterium and tritium, no greenhouse gas emissions during operation, and enhanced safety compared to fission. Though significant challenges remain, such as sustaining high-temperature plasma and achieving commercial viability, fusion holds immense promise as a critical component of the future energy mix, capable of addressing climate change while meeting the growing global demand for energy.
