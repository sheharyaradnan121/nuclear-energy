Reasons for decline

There are many reasons why nuclear energy has lost its popularity over the years. First, the costs to build a nuclear power plant are ridiculously high. It takes billions of dollars just to start a project, and on top of that, it can take years, even decades, before the plant starts generating enough electricity to make back the money spent. This long return on investment (ROI) period makes nuclear energy a risky business for investors. Another big issue is that people just don’t trust nuclear energy anymore. After events like Fukushima, the idea of nuclear reactors exploding or leaking radiation has scared a lot of people. This has led to protests and stricter government rules, making it even harder to get new plants approved. Plus, with solar and wind energy becoming so cheap and easy to set up, nuclear energy is finding it hard to compete. All these factors combined have slowed down the growth of nuclear energy worldwide.

Reason for Such a slow decline

While nuclear energy faced challenges in the West, developing nations continued to pursue it as a means to secure energy independence and meet climate goals. In the past decade, these nations have achieved significant milestones: India: India has expanded its nuclear capacity with the operationalization of new reactors like Kudankulam Units 1 and 2, with an ambitious goal of reaching 20 GW by 2030. It has also strengthened its collaboration with countries like Russia and the U.S. for advanced reactor technology and fuel supply. Pakistan: Pakistan added new nuclear reactors, including Chashma Nuclear Power Plant Units 3 and 4, with a combined capacity of over 600 MW. In 2021, the Karachi Nuclear Power Plant Unit-2 (K-2), a 1,100 MW reactor, became operational, representing a significant leap in its nuclear energy output. China: As a global leader in nuclear energy, China has consistently built and commissioned reactors, such as the Hualong One reactors, showcasing indigenous innovation. By 2023, it had over 20 GW of nuclear capacity under construction, aiming to double its capacity by 2035. South Africa: Despite economic challenges, South Africa successfully extended the operational life of the Koeberg Nuclear Power Station, the only nuclear plant in Africa, and laid groundwork for future expansion plans to stabilize its grid and reduce coal dependency.

So is it over?

The landscape of nuclear energy is shifting toward a more optimistic future: Public Sentiment: Growing awareness of climate change has softened opposition to nuclear energy, recognizing its low-carbon footprint. Economic Innovations: Small modular reactors (SMRs) offer scalable and cost-effective solutions, reducing construction risks and timelines. Safety Enhancements: Next-generation reactors incorporate passive safety systems and advanced materials to mitigate risks. Policy and International Collaboration: Efforts to streamline regulatory processes and foster global partnerships are reducing barriers to entry.

Nuclear fusion

Fusion energy—the process of mimicking the sun’s power—is seen as the holy grail of clean energy: Technological Progress: Projects like ITER and advancements in tokamak reactors demonstrate significant strides toward achieving sustained fusion reactions. Advantages Over Fission: Fusion produces minimal radioactive waste, has no meltdown risk, and uses abundant fuel sources like hydrogen isotopes. Challenges: High costs, complex engineering, and scalability remain significant hurdles, but recent breakthroughs suggest commercial viability within decades.
